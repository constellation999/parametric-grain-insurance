# Trigger-Bound Staple-Price Protection: A Multi-Layer Design for Populations Without Functioning Insurance Markets

*Concept paper — synthesizing a design discussion on decentralized insurance, oracle mechanisms, and food security*

---

## 1. System Overview

### 1.1 Objective and target risk

The system protects the *real purchasing power* of food-insecure populations against staple-price shocks. Following Sen's entitlement framework, the insured object is not the price level itself but the household's command over food — income relative to staple prices. The target population divides into two classes with opposite exposures: net food consumers (urban poor, landless laborers), who are harmed by price *increases*, and smallholder net producers, harmed by price *declines*. The system is therefore designed as a two-sided mutual from the outset.

### 1.2 Architecture: a three-layer separation result

The design follows a separation principle derived from Borch's theorem under a verifiability constraint. In a decentralized setting with no trusted adjuster, payouts must be measurable with respect to the σ-algebra generated by publicly verifiable information (the "oracle σ-algebra" 𝐺). The optimal payout is then the L² projection E[Lᵢ | 𝐺] of individual losses onto observable information; basis risk is exactly the orthogonal residual Lᵢ − E[Lᵢ | 𝐺]. This decomposition dictates the architecture:

**Layer A — Local mutual pools (residual, idiosyncratic risk).** Village- and cooperative-scale risk-sharing groups hold the fine local σ-algebra (neighbors' losses are directly observable) and enforce through social collateral. Consistent with the limited-commitment risk-sharing literature (Townsend 1994; Ligon, Thomas & Worrall 2002), these groups handle idiosyncratic risk efficiently but fail under covariate shocks that exhaust the internal pool. No blockchain component is required at this layer.

**Layer B — Index-triggered contingent claims (covariate, observable risk).** Parametric cover is written on public indices, with payouts directed *to the Layer-A pools rather than to individuals*. The aggregator then distributes internally using local information, so that basis risk — the irreducible weakness of parametric design — is absorbed by the layer holding the finest information. In the theoretical limit, this layer converges with a prediction market in state-contingent claims on public events; thin-market problems are addressed with subsidized automated market makers.

**Layer C — Tail securitization (global systemic risk).** Residual systemic exposure is transferred to global capital markets via catastrophe-bond-style, fully collateralized, index-triggered vehicles — the form traditional finance independently converged on for trust-minimized risk transfer.

### 1.3 Trigger design: multi-stage, entitlement-based

Single price triggers are dominated by a staged structure that mirrors humanitarian anticipatory-action practice:

1. **Leading stage (satellite indices):** NDVI/EVI/VCI vegetation indices (MODIS, Sentinel-2), CHIRPS precipitation, SMAP/SMOS soil moisture, and ESI evaporative stress trigger *partial early payouts* weeks to months ahead of price spikes, suppressing crisis amplification (distress asset sales).
2. **Confirming stage (entitlement ratios):** wage-to-grain and livestock-to-grain price ratios — the most direct measures of entitlement collapse (cf. the 1943 Bengal famine, where the wage-rice ratio, not aggregate supply, collapsed) — and, for import-dependent economies, an import-parity price constructed from exchange rates and world prices, both drawn from deep, manipulation-resistant markets.

Committee-judged indicators (IPC phase classifications, famine declarations) are excluded from triggers because they are systematically politicized and delayed; they reintroduce a discretionary interpretation layer that the design exists to remove. Multi-index conjunction also raises manipulation cost, since no single oracle's corruption suffices to fire a payout.

### 1.4 Settlement: partial in-kind delivery through a diversified procurement network

Because consumers' true objective is food quantity rather than cash, part of the payout is settled in kind through forward contracts with a *geographically diversified* producer network holding pre-positioned stocks. Diversification is essential: local price spikes correlate with local crop failure, so an undiversified supplier network defaults exactly when delivery is owed. Producers serve strictly as supply partners and capacity providers — never as price oracles, since a counterparty that also reports the settlement index reconstructs the conflict of interest the design eliminates. Producer-side data enters only as cross-validation against satellite and market sources.

### 1.5 Capital structure: state-priced funding

The funding principle is to assign each risk tranche to the holder for whom bearing it is cheapest — ideally negative-cost:

- **Layer 0 — Internal netting.** Consumer up-side puts and producer down-side puts partially offset within the mutual; required capital scales with *net*, not gross, exposure. Netting efficiency is maximized by spanning commodities and hemispheres (e.g., ENSO-anticorrelated regions).
- **Layer 1 — Natural hedgers.** Grain traders, exporters, and input suppliers — agents whose endowments are *long* the trigger state — sell price caps against warehouse-receipt collateral, whose value appreciates precisely when performance is owed. For sovereign exporters, contracts are financial-settlement-only with pre-posted collateral, since export bans make physical promises trigger-correlated.
- **Layer 2 — Exchange hedging.** Net systematic exposure is transferred to CME futures and options; the protocol acts as an aggregation intermediary converting retail demand into market lots.
- **Layer 3 — Concessional capital.** Donor and DFI funds enter as *first-loss tranches and contingent credit lines* (CAT-DDO-style) rather than premium subsidies, leveraging each concessional dollar into multiples of commercial capacity. Quadratic funding allocates the matching/first-loss pool across regional schemes according to the breadth of community co-contributions, and separately funds the public-goods layer: index-infrastructure refinement (which enlarges 𝐺 itself) and loss-prevention investments matched by the risk pool's own capital.

### 1.6 The narrow, honest role of blockchain

Distributed-ledger technology is *not* load-bearing for insurance economics here. Its defensible role is confined to: (i) a **trigger-bound escrow serving as a commitment device among mutually distrusting institutions** — multiple donor states, recipient governments, international agencies sharing one pre-funded pool that no party can discretionarily divert; (ii) compression of issuance costs and minimum lot sizes for Layer-C securitization; and (iii) low-friction composition of the multi-layer capital stack on a single auditable ledger. The last mile remains mobile money and aggregator agent networks.

## 2. Demand Analysis

The empirical record on index-insurance demand is sobering and directly shapes the design.

**The uptake puzzle.** Penetration of agricultural index insurance in low-income settings is persistently low — under 1% of households in nationally representative Vietnamese survey data, with even heavily supported experimental programs in India, Malawi, and Ghana reaching only 15–50% (Cole et al. 2013; Giné & Yang 2009; Karlan et al. 2014). Under expected-utility theory, risk-averse smallholders should demand actuarially fair coverage; they do not.

**Trust as the identified mechanism.** The sharpest anomaly is a *negative* correlation between risk aversion and demand, replicated across India and Ethiopia (Giné, Townsend & Vickery 2008; Cole et al. 2013; Hill, Hoddinott & Kumar 2013). Models of demand under limited trust resolve the puzzle: when payouts are subjectively uncertain — including uncertainty arising from distrust of the insurer — insurance becomes a certain premium paid for a gamble on performance, and demand can *decrease* in risk aversion. Unlike microcredit, insurance places the burden of trusting on the client rather than the institution. Consistent with the mechanism, uptake rises when products are introduced through trusted intermediaries (Cole et al. 2013), and experimental subjects who trust the index concept select higher-coverage options by roughly 10 percentage points (Kyrgyzstan lab-in-the-field evidence). Basis risk directly erodes trust dynamically: farmers who suffer losses without payouts develop durable distrust of the product class.

**The cost of the missing market.** Karlan, Osei, Osei-Akoto & Udry (2014) show in northern Ghana that offering index insurance produces strong agricultural investment responses while capital grants produce comparatively small ones — identifying *uninsured risk, not credit access, as the binding constraint* on investment. Bayesian aggregation across eight field experiments confirms substantial investment responses to insurance provision. Uninsured weather risk is further documented to push households into poverty traps through distress asset sales and costly consumption smoothing.

**Design implications.** (i) Distribution must run through trusted aggregators (cooperatives, MFIs), not direct dApp interfaces — trustlessness is meaningful only to agents with verification capacity; (ii) basis risk must be minimized visibly, via the in-kind settlement channel and the Layer-A absorption structure; (iii) early partial payouts from the satellite stage build the payout-experience track record that the trust literature identifies as the strongest demand driver; (iv) willingness to pay even small premiums is a high-information costly signal, which the QF allocation layer amplifies rather than ignores.

## 3. Incentive Analysis

**Adverse selection and moral hazard** are largely dissolved by construction: exogenous public indices leave no private-information advantage to exploit and no individual action that moves the trigger. This is the classical theoretical appeal of index insurance (Hazell 1992) and the reason the design's residual incentive problems concentrate elsewhere.

**Oracle integrity** is the binding incentive constraint. Naïve claim adjudication by capital-pool voters embeds a payout-suppressing conflict of interest (the Nexus Mutual structure); naïve prediction-market adjudication is self-referential and grants manipulators a budget equal to the claim size. The design responds by (i) choosing triggers that are *ex ante* objective (satellite feeds, deep-market prices), (ii) requiring multi-index conjunction so no single feed's corruption fires a payout, (iii) excluding all settlement-relevant reporting by interested parties, and (iv) reserving optimistic escalation games — cheap market screening with a costly trusted arbiter invoked only off-equilibrium — for any residual disputes. The interpretation layer can be compressed and pushed off-path, but not eliminated; the design's honesty consists in keeping it off the critical path.

**Capital providers.** Layer-1 natural hedgers face state-contingent marginal utility: their performance obligation binds only in states where their endowment has appreciated, so they rationally underwrite below actuarially fair prices — windfall recycling. Warehouse-receipt collateral aligns collateral value with obligation states. Layer-2 counterparties (market makers, speculators) require no mission alignment at all; the volatility risk premium is their compensation, and the system's robustness comes precisely from relying on self-interest wherever self-interest suffices, reserving scarce altruistic capital for the uninsurable tail.

**Donor commitment.** The Somalia 2011 famine is the canonical demonstration that discretion, not information, is the failure point: early-warning systems escalated for eleven months before the declaration, yet timely preventive action was not taken and approximately 258,000 people died, with donor geopolitical agendas — including U.S. anti-terrorism legislation — identified as contributing causes; historical cases (Ethiopia mid-1980s, South Sudan 1998) show deliberate withholding aligned with donor interests. The literature's summary — absent incentives for early action, preventable emergencies persist regardless of warning quality — is a commitment-layer diagnosis. Trigger-bound escrow converts donor promises from discretionary to mechanical, which is exactly the property databases under any single party's control cannot provide.

**Quadratic funding caveats.** QF applies legitimately to the system's public goods (index refinement, prevention, subsidy allocation, mutual governance via QV) but not to payouts or adjudication, which are matters of fact rather than preference. Its known failure modes are amplified in an insurance context: Sybil manipulation gains scale with √k in identity splits and here connects to direct monetary recovery, making pairwise-bounded QF, MACI-style collusion resistance, and identity attestation preconditions rather than options; the quasi-linearity assumption underlying QF's welfare theorem fails most severely for the poorest participants, requiring income-adjusted variants.

## 4. Technical Contribution

The contribution is more architectural than cryptographic.

**A measurable-selection framing of decentralized insurance.** Casting the verifiability constraint as measurability with respect to an oracle σ-algebra 𝐺 unifies the field's known pathologies: parametric insurance is conditional-expectation projection, basis risk is the orthogonal residual, claim-adjudication mechanisms are attempts to refine 𝐺 under incentive constraints, and oracle manipulation is contamination of 𝐺's generators. This framing exposes an open theoretical question — the incentive-compatible limits of σ-algebra refinement, plausibly a Myerson–Satterthwaite-type impossibility frontier between verifiability and incentive compatibility — which appears unformalized in the literature.

**Identified design gaps filled.** (i) Consumer-side staple-price protection with entitlement-ratio triggers: existing schemes (IBLI, ARC, weather index products) cover producer weather-yield risk almost exclusively; no operating scheme insures net food purchasers against price shocks on Sen-style ratio triggers. (ii) The two-sided netted mutual, exploiting the sign-opposition of producer and consumer exposures as a capital-efficiency asset. (iii) QF as the supply mechanism for oracle infrastructure — treating refinement of 𝐺 as a public good and community co-contribution as demand revelation, closing donors' information problem. (iv) A self-contained prevention loop in which the risk pool itself provides the QF matching capital for loss-prevention public goods, recovering the investment as reduced expected payouts.

**Honest placement of the ledger.** Against the field's tendency to treat decentralization as an end, the design derives blockchain's role from a constraint analysis: it strengthens only the commitment layer, only among mutually distrusting institutions, and only up to the centralization of its oracles and upgrade keys. Layer-C mechanics are explicitly a re-implementation of catastrophe bonds with compressed issuance costs, not a new principle.

## 5. Competitive Positioning

**Against traditional insurers: no advantage.** Conventional insurance rests on legal enforcement as a free trust substrate, enabling fractional reserves (versus full collateralization), fine-σ-algebra indemnity adjustment (versus basis-risk-laden indices), and regulatory trust production. Inside functioning legal systems the design is dominated on essentially every margin.

**Against existing index schemes (IBLI, ARC, cat bonds, WFP anticipatory action): narrow, specific advantages.** The component technologies all exist in non-blockchain form. The marginal contributions are (i) commitment hardness — ARC-type schemes have failed politically, not technically, through payout delays and donor non-performance, and trigger-bound escrow structurally removes the discretionary channel; (ii) one-to-two orders of magnitude compression of securitization fixed costs, bringing sub-cat-bond-scale regional pools to capital markets; (iii) low-friction composition of the four-layer capital stack on one auditable substrate; and (iv) occupation of the genuinely vacant consumer-side product space — an advantage of positioning, not of technology.

**Against the actual counterfactual: decisive advantage.** For the target population, the relevant comparison is not traditional insurance but *no insurance plus slow, discretionary ex-post aid*. Against that baseline, ex-ante priced, threshold-automatic, politically non-divertible, purchasing-power-preserving transfers are strictly superior, and the empirical costs of the status quo are measured in the Karlan et al. investment-suppression estimates on the micro side and in Somalia's 258,000 deaths on the macro side. The growing institutional shift toward anticipatory action (e.g., CERF's pre-arranged drought allocations) constitutes the humanitarian system's own admission that discretionary ex-post response has failed.

**Summary judgment.** The system has little merit as a better insurance company and substantial merit as *infrastructure for insurance-like functions where no insurance market can exist* — its advantage is a function of the institutional scarcity of trust at the deployment site. Decentralization enters the design as a constraint, never as an objective, and is accepted only at the single layer — inter-institutional commitment — where the absence of trust demonstrably binds, with body counts and reversed risk-aversion coefficients as the measurements.
